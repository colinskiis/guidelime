## Scope

This practice bulletin provides guidance on diagnosis, prevention, and treatment of venous thromboembolism (VTE) in pregnancy and postpartum.

## Key Recommendations

### Risk Factors
- Prior VTE (strongest risk factor)
- Thrombophilia (Factor V Leiden, prothrombin mutation, antiphospholipid syndrome)
- Obesity, immobilization, cesarean delivery
- Preeclampsia, postpartum hemorrhage

### Diagnosis
**DVT:**
- Compression ultrasonography
- D-dimer physiologically elevated in pregnancy—limited utility

**PE:**
- CT pulmonary angiography (CTPA) or V/Q scan
- CTPA slightly higher fetal radiation but diagnostic accuracy
- V/Q may be preferred if normal CXR

### Treatment - Acute VTE
**Anticoagulation:**
- LMWH (enoxaparin, dalteparin) preferred over UFH
- Therapeutic dosing: Enoxaparin 1 mg/kg BID
- Fondaparinux if heparin intolerance
- DOACs contraindicated in pregnancy

**Duration:**
- Continue through pregnancy and ≥6 weeks postpartum (minimum 3 months total)

**Delivery Planning:**
- Hold LMWH 24 hours before planned delivery
- Consider UFH near term for flexibility
- Neuraxial anesthesia timing considerations

### Thromboprophylaxis
**Antepartum:**
- Prior unprovoked VTE: Prophylactic or intermediate-dose LMWH
- Prior VTE + thrombophilia: Prophylactic or therapeutic anticoagulation
- High-risk thrombophilia without VTE: Prophylactic LMWH

**Postpartum (High-Risk):**
- Prior VTE: 6 weeks anticoagulation postpartum
- Cesarean delivery risk factors: Consider short-term prophylaxis

### Mechanical Prophylaxis
- Compression stockings
- Intermittent pneumatic compression during cesarean

### Bridging Around Delivery
- Detailed planning for anticoagulation peripartum
- Balance bleeding vs. thrombotic risk
