## Scope
2024 ACOG practice bulletin on the prediction, prevention, and management of shoulder dystocia.

## Key Recommendations

### Definition
- Shoulder dystocia occurs when the fetal shoulders fail to deliver after delivery of the head, requiring additional maneuvers beyond gentle downward traction.

### Risk Factors
- **Fetal Macrosomia**: Most significant risk factor, especially with maternal diabetes.
- Prior shoulder dystocia.
- Maternal obesity, excessive weight gain.
- Prolonged second stage, operative vaginal delivery.
- Post-term pregnancy.

### Prediction
- Shoulder dystocia cannot be reliably predicted or prevented.
- Estimated fetal weight is imprecise.

### Prevention Strategies
- **Cesarean Delivery**: Consider if EFW ≥5,000 g (non-diabetic) or ≥4,500 g (diabetic). Otherwise, routine prophylactic cesarean for macrosomia is NOT recommended.
- **Induction for Suspected Macrosomia**: May be offered; evidence for benefit is limited.

### Management (Maneuvers in Sequence)

1. **Call for Help**: Additional personnel, neonatology.
2. **McRoberts Maneuver**: Hyperflexion of maternal thighs onto abdomen – first-line.
3. **Suprapubic Pressure**: Applied by assistant to dislodge anterior shoulder.
4. **Rotational Maneuvers (Rubin II, Woods Screw)**: Rotate fetal shoulders to oblique diameter.
5. **Posterior Arm Delivery**: Deliver posterior arm first.
6. **All-Fours Position (Gaskin Maneuver)**: Patient on hands and knees.
7. **Zavanelli Maneuver / Symphysiotomy**: Last resort if all else fails.

### Complications
- Brachial plexus injury (usually transient Erb's palsy).
- Clavicle or humeral fracture.
- Hypoxic-ischemic injury (rare if managed promptly).
- Maternal lacerations, postpartum hemorrhage.

### Documentation
- Document time from head to body delivery, maneuvers used, outcome.
