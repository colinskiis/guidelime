## Scope

This guideline provides updated recommendations for the diagnosis and management of gout, including lifestyle, pharmacologic, and treat-to-target strategies.

## Key Recommendations

### Diagnosis
- Crystal identification (synovial fluid or tophus) remains gold standard
- Dual-energy CT if aspiration not feasible
- Clinical diagnosis with characteristic presentation acceptable
- Serum urate may be normal during acute flares

### Acute Flare Management
**First-Line Options:**
- Colchicine (low-dose: 1mg then 0.5mg 1 hour later)
- NSAIDs at full dose
- Oral corticosteroids (prednisolone 30-35mg x 3-5 days)

**Combination Therapy:**
- For severe flares or refractory cases
- Intra-articular corticosteroid for monoarticular

**IL-1 Inhibitors:**
- Anakinra for refractory acute flares (off-label)
- Canakinumab in selected cases

### Urate-Lowering Therapy (ULT)
**Indications:**
- Recurrent flares (≥2/year)
- Tophi or urate arthropathy
- Uric acid stones
- Consider in first flare with high urate, young age, comorbidities

**Treatment:**
- Allopurinol first-line (start low 100mg, titrate to target)
- Febuxostat alternative (40-120mg)
- Probenecid for underexcretors

### Treat-to-Target
- Target serum urate <6 mg/dL (360 µmol/L)
- Target <5 mg/dL for severe gout with tophi
- Lifelong therapy usually required

### Flare Prophylaxis During ULT Initiation
- Colchicine 0.5-1mg daily for 3-6 months
- Low-dose NSAID or prednisolone alternatives

### Lifestyle
- Weight loss and dietary modifications
- Limit alcohol (especially beer) and high-fructose beverages
- Limit purine-rich foods
- Adequate hydration
