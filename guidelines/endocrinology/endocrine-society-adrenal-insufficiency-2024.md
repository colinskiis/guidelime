## Scope
2024 Endocrine Society guideline update on the diagnosis and management of primary adrenal insufficiency (Addison's disease).

## Key Recommendations

### Diagnosis

#### Clinical Suspicion
- Fatigue, weight loss, anorexia, nausea, hyperpigmentation (primary AI), hypotension, salt craving, hyponatremia, hyperkalemia.

#### Biochemical Testing
- **Morning Serum Cortisol**: <3 μg/dL strongly suggests AI; >15-18 μg/dL makes it unlikely.
- **ACTH Stimulation Test (Cosyntropin 250 μg)**: Peak cortisol <18 μg/dL at 30-60 min confirms AI.
- **Plasma ACTH**: Elevated in primary AI (vs. low/normal in secondary).

#### Etiology
- Autoimmune adrenalitis (most common in developed countries).
- Infectious (TB, fungal in endemic areas).
- Adrenal hemorrhage, metastases, drugs (ketoconazole, etomidate, immune checkpoint inhibitors).
- Congenital adrenal hyperplasia, adrenoleukodystrophy.

### Treatment

#### Glucocorticoid Replacement
- **Hydrocortisone**: 15-25 mg/day in divided doses (typically 10-15 mg on waking, 5 mg midday, ± 5 mg late afternoon). Mimics diurnal rhythm.
- **Alternatives**: Prednisone 3-5 mg/day or prednisolone if simpler dosing desired.

#### Mineralocorticoid Replacement (Primary AI Only)
- **Fludrocortisone**: 50-200 μg/day.
- Adjust based on BP, electrolytes, and plasma renin activity.

### Sick Day Rules / Stress Dosing
- Double or triple glucocorticoid dose for febrile illness, vomiting, surgery, trauma.
- IM hydrocortisone injection for emergencies.
- All patients should carry a medical alert and emergency injection kit.

### Adrenal Crisis
- Hypotension, shock, hyponatremia, hyperkalemia, altered consciousness.
- **Treatment**: IV hydrocortisone 100 mg bolus, then 50-100 mg every 6-8 hours + IV fluid resuscitation.

### Monitoring
- Clinical assessment (energy, BP, weight), electrolytes, morning cortisol (if timing allows), and avoidance of over-replacement (Cushing features, osteoporosis).
