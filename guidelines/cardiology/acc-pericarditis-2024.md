## Scope
2024 ACC expert consensus on the diagnosis and management of pericarditis and pericardial syndromes.

## Key Recommendations

### Diagnosis
- **Clinical Criteria**: ≥2 of: Chest pain (pleuritic, improved sitting forward), pericardial friction rub, ECG changes (diffuse ST elevation, PR depression), pericardial effusion on imaging.
- **Labs**: CRP, troponin (mild elevation in myopericarditis).
- **Echocardiography**: Assess effusion, tamponade, constrictive features.
- **CT/MRI**: For complications, constrictive pericarditis, or etiology workup.

### Etiology
- Most cases are idiopathic (presumed viral) in developed countries.
- Consider specific causes: TB (endemic areas), autoimmune, malignancy, post-MI (Dressler), post-cardiac surgery, uremia.

### Acute Pericarditis Treatment
- **NSAIDs (First-Line)**: Ibuprofen 600 mg TID or aspirin 750-1000 mg TID x 1-2 weeks, then taper.
- **Colchicine (Add to NSAID)**: 0.5 mg BID (or daily if <70 kg) x 3 months. Reduces recurrence. Strongly recommended.
- **Corticosteroids**: Avoid initial use if possible (increases recurrence risk). Reserve for contraindication to NSAIDs/colchicine or specific etiology (autoimmune).

### Recurrent Pericarditis
- Continue colchicine for ≥6 months.
- Low-dose corticosteroids if refractory.
- **Anakinra (IL-1 Receptor Antagonist)**: For corticosteroid-dependent or colchicine-resistant recurrent pericarditis. Highly effective.
- **Azathioprine, IVIG**: Alternatives.
- **Pericardiectomy**: Last resort for frequent, debilitating recurrences.

### Pericardial Effusion / Tamponade
- **Pericardiocentesis**: For tamponade or diagnostic purposes.
- Treat underlying cause.

### Constrictive Pericarditis
- Differentiate from restrictive cardiomyopathy (echo, CT, MRI, hemodynamics).
- **Pericardiectomy**: Definitive treatment for symptomatic constriction.
